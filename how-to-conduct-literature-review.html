How to Conduct Your Literature Review

A step-by-step guide — from understanding what a literature review is, to identifying sources, extracting findings, and spotting research gaps.

What you are actually doing

A literature review is not a summary of articles. It is an organised, analytical account of what researchers have already found about your topic and its variables. Your job is to show how previous studies relate to each other — and to identify what has not yet been answered.

🎯 The goal: By the end of your literature review, a reader should understand (1) how your variables have been defined and measured, (2) what findings already exist, and (3) why your specific study is still needed.

What counts as valid literature

  • ✅ Peer-reviewed journal articles (primary source — always preferred)
  • ✅ Book chapters from academic publishers (e.g. Springer, Elsevier, Routledge, Sage)
  • ❌ Blogs, personal websites, or internet news articles — never cite these
  • ❌ Wikipedia or encyclopedias — not peer-reviewed, not citable
  • ❌ Unpublished theses unless from a verified institutional repository
Reject any source that: has no named author, has no year of publication, is not hosted on an academic platform, or cannot be found via Google Scholar.

Publication window

Use sources published between the last 10 years and the current year (). Older foundational theories (e.g. TAM, Ajzen's TPB) may be cited if they are the original source of a concept — but must be acknowledged as seminal works, not recent evidence.

How many sources do you need?

  • Download 35–45 articles at the start (your candidate pool)
  • After reading and assessing relevance, keep 20–25 for your final review
  • Fewer than 15 sources is insufficient for a Research Methodology literature review

Why criteria must be defined before you search

Without predefined criteria, students pick articles they happen to like or ones that are easy to understand. That is selection bias. Define your criteria first — then apply them consistently to every article you find.

Inclusion criteria — include an article ONLY if:

  • It is a peer-reviewed journal article or an academic book chapter
  • It is published within the last 10 years (or is a named seminal work)
  • It directly addresses at least one of your variables: your independent, dependent, or control variables
  • The full text is available (not just an abstract)
  • It has a clearly stated research method (quantitative, qualitative, or mixed)

Exclusion criteria — exclude an article if:

  • It is a blog post, news article, opinion column, or website content
  • It has no identifiable author or publication year
  • It is not peer-reviewed (e.g. working papers without institutional backing)
  • The full text is behind a paywall you cannot access via your institution
  • It addresses a different context entirely (e.g. a study on industrial machinery when your topic is consumer behaviour)
📋 Document your criteria. Write them down before you begin searching. Your supervisor may ask you to justify why certain articles were excluded.

Organise your articles into two folders

PrimaryArticlesDownloaded/ ← everything you downloaded (35–45 articles) SelectedArticles/ ← articles that passed your criteria (20–25 articles) Rule: Copy articles into SelectedArticles — do NOT move or cut-paste from PrimaryArticlesDownloaded.

Why keywords matter

Academic databases do not read sentences. They match keywords. If you search with vague or incorrect terms, you miss relevant literature. You need systematic keyword combinations — not a single search phrase.

Use Boolean operators: AND (narrows results — both terms must appear) · OR (broadens results — either term appears) · NOT (excludes a term). Always combine at least two of your core terms per search.

Where to search

  • Scopus — scopus.com
  • JSTOR — jstor.org
  • ResearchGate — researchgate.net
  • Semantic Scholar — semanticscholar.org
⚠️ Never search only once. Run at least 6–8 different keyword combinations across at least 2 databases. Record every search string you use.

How to read an academic article efficiently

Do not read from start to finish on the first pass. Use the strategic reading sequence below:

  1. Title and abstract — decides in 60 seconds whether this article belongs in your pool
  2. Introduction — identifies the research problem and variables
  3. Literature Review section — shows you how others defined and measured your variables
  4. Methodology section — reveals sample size, data collection method, statistical tools used
  5. Results and Discussion — the actual findings: what relationships were found, how strong
  6. Conclusion and limitations — what the authors say still needs to be studied (these are your research gaps)

Highlighting — colour-coded system

  • 🟡 Yellow — definitions of your variables
  • 🔵 Blue — measurement tools and scales used
  • 🟢 Green — key findings and statistical results
  • 🔴 Red / Pink — research gaps mentioned by the authors
⚠️ Save after every session — Press Ctrl+S after adding highlights. Do not rely on auto-save.

The extraction document

Create a single shared document named: ExtractionDoc_[GroupName].docx

For each article, record:

  1. Variable Definitions — how each variable is defined in this article
  2. Measurement / Scale — what instrument or scale was used
  3. Methodology Used — quantitative / qualitative / mixed; sample size; country
  4. Key Findings — what the study found (include statistics if available)
  5. Research Gaps — what the authors said still needs investigation
💡 Summarise each section in 2–4 sentences in your own words. Copying verbatim is plagiarism even in a working document.

What is a research gap?

A research gap is a question that existing literature has not fully answered. Your study must be justified by at least one clear gap.

Three types of gaps to look for

  • Contextual gap — Your study is set in a different or under-researched context — this is a valid gap.
  • Variable gap — The relationship between your independent variable and your dependent variable has not been studied together in existing literature.
  • Methodological gap — Prior studies used qualitative methods; your study applies quantitative testing. Or vice versa.
✍️
How to Write Your Gap Statement

End your literature review with a dedicated paragraph naming the gap your study addresses:

"Despite substantial research on [your topic], limited studies have examined…"

Complete the sentence by naming the specific relationship, context, or variable combination that prior studies have not addressed.

Contradictions in the literature

If two studies found opposite results, highlight it. Contradictions are themselves a justification for further research.

Final completion checklist

  • ☐ Inclusion and exclusion criteria documented before searching
  • ☐ Keyword combinations generated and recorded
  • ☐ 35–45 articles downloaded into PrimaryArticlesDownloaded
  • ☐ All articles screened; 20–25 copied to SelectedArticles
  • ☐ All selected articles read using the strategic reading sequence
  • ☐ All articles highlighted and saved
  • ☐ Extraction document completed with all five sections per article
  • ☐ Methodology comparison table filled
  • ☐ At least 3 research gaps identified and recorded
📌 Once all items above are done, you are ready to write the literature review section of your research report.
